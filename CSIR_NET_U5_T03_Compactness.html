Introduction

Compactness generalizes "closed and bounded" from ℝⁿ to abstract spaces. A compact space is one where "every open cover has a finite subcover" — a finiteness condition that has powerful analytical consequences.

Definitions of Compactness

Open Cover / Compact

An open cover of X is a collection {Uα} of open sets with X ⊆ ⋃Uα.

(X,d) is compact if every open cover has a finite subcover.

Sequential Compactness

(X,d) is sequentially compact if every sequence has a convergent subsequence.

In metric spaces: compact ⟺ sequentially compact.

Countable Compactness

Every countable open cover has a finite subcover. In metric spaces this is equivalent to compactness.

Characterization in Metric Spaces

A metric space is compact iff it is complete and totally bounded.

This gives three equivalent notions for metric spaces: compact ↔ sequentially compact ↔ complete + totally bounded.

Heine-Borel Theorem

Heine-Borel Theorem (ℝⁿ)

A subset K ⊆ ℝⁿ is compact (in the Euclidean topology) iff K is closed and bounded.

Fails in Infinite Dimensions

In infinite-dimensional normed spaces (e.g., ℓ², C[0,1]), closed and bounded ≠ compact. The closed unit ball of ℓ² is closed and bounded but NOT compact (the sequence of standard basis vectors eₙ has no convergent subsequence).

Properties of Compact Sets

Key Properties
  • Every compact subset of a metric space is closed and bounded.
  • A closed subset of a compact space is compact.
  • Finite unions and arbitrary intersections of compact sets are compact.
  • The continuous image of a compact set is compact.
  • A compact metric space is separable (has a countable dense subset).
  • A compact metric space has the Bolzano-Weierstrass property: every infinite subset has a limit point.
Lebesgue Number Lemma

If {Uα} is an open cover of a compact metric space X, there exists δ>0 (Lebesgue number) such that every subset of X with diameter < δ is contained in some Uα.

Applications of Compactness

Extreme Value Theorem

If f: K→ℝ is continuous and K is compact, then f attains its maximum and minimum on K.

Proof: f(K) is compact in ℝ, hence closed and bounded. Therefore sup f(K) ∈ f(K).

Heine-Cantor Theorem

Every continuous function f: K→Y where K is compact is uniformly continuous.

Key step: use the Lebesgue number lemma for the cover {f⁻¹(B(y,ε/2))}.

Arzelà-Ascoli Theorem

A subset F ⊆ C[a,b] is compact (in sup norm) iff F is bounded and equicontinuous.

Equicontinuous: ∀ε>0, ∃δ>0: |x−y|<δ ⟹ |f(x)−f(y)|<ε for ALL f∈F.

Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem (ℝⁿ)

Every bounded sequence in ℝⁿ has a convergent subsequence. This follows from compactness of closed bounded subsets of ℝⁿ.
