Introduction

Group theory is the mathematical study of symmetry. A group is an abstract set equipped with a binary operation satisfying four axioms. Groups arise everywhere: symmetries of geometric objects, permutations, number systems, and quantum mechanics.

Groups: Basic Definitions

Group

A group (G, ·) is a set G with a binary operation · satisfying:

  1. Closure: a·b ∈ G for all a,b ∈ G
  2. Associativity: (a·b)·c = a·(b·c)
  3. Identity: ∃e ∈ G such that e·a = a·e = a for all a
  4. Inverses: ∀a ∈ G, ∃a⁻¹ ∈ G such that a·a⁻¹ = a⁻¹·a = e

If also a·b = b·a for all a,b, G is called abelian (commutative).

Basic Properties

  • The identity element e is unique.
  • Each element has a unique inverse.
  • Cancellation law: ab=ac ⟹ b=c (left); ba=ca ⟹ b=c (right).
  • (ab)⁻¹ = b⁻¹a⁻¹ (reverse order).
  • (a⁻¹)⁻¹ = a.
Order of an Element

The order of element g ∈ G, denoted ord(g) or o(g), is the smallest positive integer n such that gⁿ = e. If no such n exists, g has infinite order.

In ℤ₁₂: ord(4) = 3 (since 4+4+4=12≡0), ord(1) = 12, ord(6) = 2.

Subgroups & Cosets

Subgroup

H ≤ G (H is a subgroup of G) if H ⊆ G and H itself is a group under the same operation.

Subgroup test: H ≤ G ⟺ H ≠ ∅, and for all a,b ∈ H: ab⁻¹ ∈ H.

Trivial Subgroups

Every group G has at least two subgroups: {e} (trivial) and G itself.

A group with no proper subgroups is either {e} or has prime order (cyclic of prime order).

Cosets

Left and Right Cosets

For H ≤ G and g ∈ G:

  • Left coset: gH = {gh : h ∈ H}
  • Right coset: Hg = {hg : h ∈ H}

The collection of all left cosets {gH : g ∈ G} partitions G.

Key Facts about Cosets
  • |gH| = |H| for all g ∈ G (all cosets have the same size).
  • Two left cosets are either equal or disjoint.
  • g ∈ gH always (since e ∈ H).
  • g₁H = g₂H ⟺ g₁⁻¹g₂ ∈ H.

Lagrange's Theorem

Lagrange's Theorem

If G is a finite group and H ≤ G, then |H| divides |G|. Moreover:

|G| = |H| · [G : H]

where [G:H] = number of distinct cosets = index of H in G.

Proof sketch

The cosets partition G into equal-sized parts, each of size |H|. If there are [G:H] cosets, then |G| = |H|·[G:H].

Consequences of Lagrange's Theorem

  • The order of every element divides |G|: ord(g) | |G|.
  • Every group of prime order p is cyclic and has no proper subgroups.
  • If |G| = p (prime), G ≅ ℤₚ.
  • Fermat's little theorem: aᵖ ≡ a (mod p) for prime p. (From aᵖ⁻¹≡1 for gcd(a,p)=1.)
  • Euler's theorem: a^(φ(n)) ≡ 1 (mod n) for gcd(a,n)=1.
Converse is FALSE

If k | |G|, there need not be a subgroup of order k. Example: A₄ has order 12 but no subgroup of order 6.

However: Cauchy's theorem guarantees a subgroup of order p for every prime p | |G|.

Cyclic Groups

Cyclic Group

G is cyclic if G = ⟨g⟩ = {gⁿ : n ∈ ℤ} for some g ∈ G (called a generator).

ℤₙ is cyclic of order n generated by 1. ℤ is infinite cyclic generated by 1 (or −1).

Structure of Cyclic Groups
  • Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic.
  • If G = ⟨g⟩ has order n, then ⟨gᵏ⟩ has order n/gcd(k,n).
  • ℤₙ has exactly one subgroup of order d for each d | n.
  • The number of generators of ℤₙ is φ(n) (Euler's totient).
Classification of Cyclic Groups
  • Every infinite cyclic group is isomorphic to (ℤ, +).
  • Every cyclic group of order n is isomorphic to (ℤₙ, +).
  • ℤₘₙ ≅ ℤₘ × ℤₙ if and only if gcd(m,n) = 1.

Permutation Groups

Symmetric Group Sₙ

Sₙ = group of all permutations of {1,2,...,n} under composition. |Sₙ| = n!.

Every permutation decomposes uniquely (up to order) into disjoint cycles.

Cycle Notation & Sign

Example in S₅

σ = (1 3 5)(2 4): sends 1→3→5→1 and 2→4→2. This is a product of a 3-cycle and a 2-cycle.

Order of σ = lcm(3,2) = 6.

Sign (Parity) of a Permutation

Every permutation is a product of transpositions. The sign sgn(σ) = (−1)^k where k = number of transpositions.

  • Even permutation: sgn(σ) = +1 (product of even # transpositions)
  • Odd permutation: sgn(σ) = −1
  • A k-cycle has sign (−1)^(k−1): 2-cycles odd, 3-cycles even.
Alternating Group Aₙ

Aₙ = {σ ∈ Sₙ : sgn(σ) = 1} = group of even permutations.

|Aₙ| = n!/2. A₅ has order 60 and is the smallest non-abelian simple group.

Cayley's Theorem

Every group G is isomorphic to a subgroup of some symmetric group. Specifically G ↪ S_{|G|}.
