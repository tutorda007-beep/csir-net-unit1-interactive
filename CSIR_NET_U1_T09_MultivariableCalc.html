Unit 1 — Analysis · Topic 09 of 11
Multivariable Calculus
Partial derivatives, total differentiability, Jacobian, Implicit & Inverse Function Theorems, and constrained optimization via Lagrange multipliers
CSIR NET High-Yield Several Variables Advanced 80 min Study

Learning Objectives

  1. Compute partial derivatives and understand when mixed partials are equal (Clairaut's theorem)
  2. Define total differentiability and distinguish it from existence of partial derivatives
  3. Compute the Jacobian matrix and apply the multivariable chain rule
  4. State and apply the Implicit Function Theorem (IFT) and Inverse Function Theorem (IvFT)
  5. Find constrained extrema using Lagrange multipliers
🎯
CSIR NET Frequency: IFT and Jacobian appear in 2–3 questions per exam. Critical: "when can we solve F(x,y)=0 for y as function of x?" → IFT. Lagrange multipliers for constrained optimization. Clairaut's theorem on mixed partials.

∂ Partial Derivatives

∂
Concept 1
Partial Derivatives and Higher-Order Partials
Partial Derivative
∂f/∂x (x₀, y₀) = lim_{h→0} [f(x₀+h, y₀) − f(x₀, y₀)] / h

Differentiate with respect to x while treating y as a constant. Similarly define ∂f/∂y.
Clairaut's (Schwarz's) Theorem on Mixed Partials
If f : ℝ² → ℝ has continuous second partial derivatives in a neighborhood of (a,b), then:

∂²f/∂x∂y (a,b) = ∂²f/∂y∂x (a,b)

The order of differentiation can be swapped when mixed partials are continuous.
Counterexample when NOT continuous: f(x,y) = xy(x²−y²)/(x²+y²) at (0,0). Here ∂²f/∂x∂y(0,0) = 1 but ∂²f/∂y∂x(0,0) = −1. So continuity of mixed partials is essential.

Higher-Order Notation

NotationMeaning
∂²f/∂x² = fₓₓSecond derivative w.r.t. x twice
∂²f/∂x∂y = fₓᵧDifferentiate w.r.t. y first, then x
∂²f/∂y∂x = fᵧₓDifferentiate w.r.t. x first, then y
Hessian H(f)Matrix of all second partial derivatives
⚡
Existence of partials ≠ differentiability! A function can have all partial derivatives at a point but still fail to be differentiable there. Example: f(x,y) = xy/√(x²+y²) at (0,0) — both partials = 0 but f is not differentiable at (0,0). Differentiability requires the total derivative to exist.

∇ Total Differentiability

Df
Concept 2
Total Derivative and Gradient
Total Differentiability at a Point
f : ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ is differentiable at a if there exists a linear map L: ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ such that:

lim_{h→0} [f(a+h) − f(a) − L(h)] / ‖h‖ = 0

L is the total derivative (Fréchet derivative) of f at a, denoted Df(a). For f: ℝⁿ → ℝ, L(h) = ∇f(a) · h.
Differentiability Sufficient Condition
If all partial derivatives ∂f/∂xᵢ exist and are continuous in a neighborhood of a, then f is differentiable at a.
Existence + continuity of partials → differentiable. But: differentiable → partials exist (not necessarily continuous). Mere existence of partials without continuity is NOT enough for differentiability.

Hierarchy of Smoothness

C¹ (Continuously Differentiable)

All partial derivatives exist and are continuous. C¹ ⟹ differentiable ⟹ continuous.

C² (Twice Continuously Differentiable)

All second partial derivatives exist and are continuous. C² ⟹ Clairaut's theorem holds (fₓᵧ = fᵧₓ).

Differentiable (but not C¹)

All partials exist, total derivative exists, but partials may not be continuous. This is the "gap" between existence and smoothness.

Partials exist (but not differentiable)

Partials can exist at a point without the function being differentiable there. The partial derivatives "see" only along coordinate directions.

Directional Derivative
The directional derivative of f at a in direction u (unit vector) is:
Dᵤf(a) = lim_{t→0} [f(a+tu) − f(a)]/t = ∇f(a) · u (if f is differentiable)

If f is only Gâteaux differentiable (directional derivatives exist in all directions), f need NOT be Fréchet differentiable.

J Chain Rule & Jacobian

J
Concept 3
Jacobian Matrix and Chain Rule
Jacobian Matrix
For f = (f₁,…,fₘ) : ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ, the Jacobian matrix at a is the m×n matrix:

J_f(a) = [∂fᵢ/∂xⱼ] (i = 1,…,m; j = 1,…,n)

The Jacobian determinant det(J_f) (when m=n) measures how volumes scale under the transformation.
Multivariable Chain Rule
If g: ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ is differentiable at a, and f: ℝᵐ → ℝᵏ is differentiable at g(a), then f∘g is differentiable at a with:

J_{f∘g}(a) = J_f(g(a)) · J_g(a) (matrix multiplication)

Special case (f: ℝ²→ℝ, x=x(t), y=y(t)):
df/dt = (∂f/∂x)(dx/dt) + (∂f/∂y)(dy/dt)

Change of Variables in Integration

Change of Variables Formula
If T: U → V is a C¹ diffeomorphism (bijective with C¹ inverse), then:

∫_V f(x) dx = ∫_U f(T(u)) |det J_T(u)| du

Examples: polar (r,θ)→(x,y): |J| = r. Spherical (r,θ,φ)→(x,y,z): |J| = r²sin(φ).
CoordinatesTransformationJacobian |J|
Polar (2D)x=r cos θ, y=r sin θr
Cylindrical (3D)x=r cos θ, y=r sin θ, z=zr
Spherical (3D)x=ρ sin φ cos θ, y=ρ sin φ sin θ, z=ρ cos φρ² sin φ

⚙️ Implicit & Inverse Function Theorems

IFT
Concept 4
IFT and Inverse Function Theorem
Implicit Function Theorem (IFT)
Let F: ℝⁿ × ℝᵐ → ℝᵐ be C¹ near (a, b) with F(a,b) = 0. If the partial Jacobian ∂F/∂y at (a,b) is invertible (det ≠ 0), then there exist neighborhoods U of a and V of b, and a unique C¹ function g: U → V such that:

F(x, g(x)) = 0 for all x ∈ U, with g(a) = b.

Moreover: ∂g/∂x = −(∂F/∂y)⁻¹ · (∂F/∂x)
Simplest case: F(x,y) = 0 in ℝ². If ∂F/∂y ≠ 0 at (a,b), then near (a,b), the equation F(x,y)=0 defines y as a C¹ function of x. dy/dx = −(∂F/∂x)/(∂F/∂y).
Inverse Function Theorem (IvFT)
Let f: ℝⁿ → ℝⁿ be C¹ near a, with det J_f(a) ≠ 0 (invertible Jacobian). Then there exist neighborhoods U of a and V of f(a) such that:
(i) f: U → V is a bijection
(ii) The inverse f⁻¹: V → U is C¹
(iii) J_{f⁻¹}(f(a)) = [J_f(a)]⁻¹ (inverse of the Jacobian matrix)
IFT ⟺ IvFT: they are equivalent theorems. IvFT says: non-singular Jacobian → locally invertible.

IFT Summary Table

ConditionConclusion
F(a,b) = 0, ∂F/∂y(a,b) ≠ 0Can solve y = g(x) locally near (a,b)
∂F/∂y(a,b) = 0IFT gives no information; solution curve may not be a function
det J_f(a) ≠ 0f is locally invertible near a (IvFT)
det J_f(a) = 0f may not be locally invertible

⊕ Extrema & Lagrange Multipliers

max
Concept 5
Critical Points, Hessian, Lagrange Multipliers
Second Derivative Test for f(x,y)
At a critical point (fₓ = fᵧ = 0), compute D = fₓₓ fᵧᵧ − (fₓᵧ)² = det(Hessian):

• D > 0 and fₓₓ > 0: Local minimum
• D > 0 and fₓₓ < 0: Local maximum
• D < 0: Saddle point
• D = 0: Inconclusive (higher-order test needed)
Lagrange Multipliers
To find extrema of f(x,y) subject to constraint g(x,y) = 0:

Solve the system: ∇f = λ∇g and g(x,y) = 0

∂f/∂x = λ·∂g/∂x, ∂f/∂y = λ·∂g/∂y, g(x,y) = 0

The constant λ is the Lagrange multiplier. This works when ∇g ≠ 0 at the constrained optimum.
Lagrange Example
Minimize f(x,y) = x² + y² subject to x + y = 1.
g(x,y) = x + y − 1 = 0. ∇f = (2x, 2y) = λ(1,1) = λ∇g.
From ∇f = λ∇g: 2x = λ and 2y = λ ⟹ x = y. With x+y=1: 2x=1 ⟹ x=y=1/2.
Minimum value: f(1/2, 1/2) = 1/4 + 1/4 = 1/2.

✏️ Worked Examples

Example 1 — Existence of Partials ≠ Differentiability
Let f(x,y) = xy/√(x²+y²) for (x,y) ≠ (0,0), f(0,0) = 0. Show fₓ(0,0) = fᵧ(0,0) = 0 but f is NOT differentiable at (0,0).
fₓ(0,0) = lim_{h→0} [f(h,0)−f(0,0)]/h = lim_{h→0} 0/|h| = 0. Similarly fᵧ(0,0) = 0.
For differentiability, need: [f(h,k)−f(0,0)−0·h−0·k]/‖(h,k)‖ → 0.
Take h = k: f(h,h) = h²/√(2h²) = h²/(|h|√2) = |h|/√2.
[f(h,h)−0]/(‖(h,h)‖) = (|h|/√2)/(|h|√2) = 1/2 ≠ 0. So NOT differentiable at (0,0). □
Example 2 — Jacobian Determinant
For the polar coordinate transformation T(r,θ) = (r cos θ, r sin θ), compute det J_T.
J_T = [[∂(r cos θ)/∂r, ∂(r cos θ)/∂θ], [∂(r sin θ)/∂r, ∂(r sin θ)/∂θ]]
= [[cos θ, −r sin θ], [sin θ, r cos θ]]
det J_T = cos θ · (r cos θ) − (−r sin θ)(sin θ) = r cos²θ + r sin²θ = r. □
This gives ∫∫_D f(x,y) dA = ∫∫ f(r cos θ, r sin θ) · r dr dθ.
Example 3 — Applying IFT
Near (x,y) = (0,1): can we solve F(x,y) = x² + y³ − 1 = 0 for y as a function of x?
F(0,1) = 0 + 1 − 1 = 0. ✓
∂F/∂y = 3y² at (0,1): = 3(1)² = 3 ≠ 0.
By IFT: yes, we can solve y = g(x) near (0,1), where g is C¹.
dy/dx = −(∂F/∂x)/(∂F/∂y) = −2x/(3y²). At (0,1): dy/dx = 0.
Example 4 — Saddle Point
Classify the critical points of f(x,y) = x² − y².
fₓ = 2x = 0, fᵧ = −2y = 0 ⟹ (0,0) is the only critical point.
Hessian: fₓₓ = 2, fᵧᵧ = −2, fₓᵧ = 0.
D = fₓₓ fᵧᵧ − (fₓᵧ)² = (2)(−2) − 0 = −4 < 0.
Saddle point at (0,0). (f increases along x-axis but decreases along y-axis.)
Example 5 — Inverse Function Theorem
For f(x,y) = (x+y, x−y), show f has a C¹ inverse near any point. Find J_{f⁻¹}.
J_f = [[1,1],[1,−1]]. det J_f = −1−1 = −2 ≠ 0.
By IvFT, f is locally (in fact globally) invertible. J_{f⁻¹} = (J_f)⁻¹ = (1/−2)[[−1,−1],[−1,1]] = [[1/2, 1/2],[1/2, −1/2]].
Check: f⁻¹(u,v) = ((u+v)/2, (u−v)/2). Verify: f(f⁻¹(u,v)) = ((u+v)/2+(u−v)/2, (u+v)/2−(u−v)/2) = (u, v). ✓

Question 1 of 15
Clairaut's theorem states that fₓᵧ = fᵧₓ at (a,b) when:
✓ C is correct. Clairaut's theorem: if fₓᵧ and fᵧₓ both exist and are continuous in a neighborhood of (a,b), then fₓᵧ(a,b) = fᵧₓ(a,b). Continuity of mixed partials is sufficient. The theorem fails when mixed partials are discontinuous: the classic counterexample has fₓᵧ(0,0) ≠ fᵧₓ(0,0).
Question 2 of 15
Existence of all partial derivatives at a point a implies:
✓ C is correct. The existence of partial derivatives is a very weak condition — it only looks along coordinate directions. Example: f(x,y) = xy/(x²+y²) for (x,y)≠(0,0), f(0,0)=0. Partials ∂f/∂x(0,0)=0 and ∂f/∂y(0,0)=0 both exist, but f is not continuous at (0,0) (along y=x: f=1/2). Continuous partials → differentiable → continuous.
Question 3 of 15
The Jacobian of the polar transformation (r,θ) → (x,y) = (r cos θ, r sin θ) is:
✓ C is correct. J = [[cos θ, −r sin θ],[sin θ, r cos θ]]. det J = r cos²θ + r sin²θ = r. This is why ∫∫ f(x,y) dx dy = ∫∫ f(r cos θ, r sin θ)·r dr dθ. The extra factor of r comes from the Jacobian of the polar transformation.
Question 4 of 15
The Implicit Function Theorem guarantees y = g(x) locally near (a,b) with F(a,b)=0 when:
✓ B is correct. IFT: to solve F(x,y) = 0 for y = g(x), the key condition is ∂F/∂y ≠ 0 (the variable we're solving for). The formula is dy/dx = −(∂F/∂x)/(∂F/∂y). A is wrong: ∂F/∂x being nonzero lets us solve for x as function of y, not y as function of x. Also F must be C¹ (or at least with continuous partial derivatives).
Question 5 of 15
For f(x,y) = x³ + y³ − 3xy, the critical points are:
✓ B is correct. fₓ = 3x² − 3y = 0 ⟹ y = x². fᵧ = 3y² − 3x = 0 ⟹ x = y². Substituting: x = (x²)² = x⁴ ⟹ x⁴ − x = 0 ⟹ x(x³−1) = 0 ⟹ x = 0 or x = 1. So (x,y) = (0,0) or (1,1). Hessian at (1,1): D = fₓₓfᵧᵧ − fₓᵧ² = (6)(6)−(−3)² = 36−9=27>0, fₓₓ=6>0 → local min. At (0,0): D = 0·0−(−3)² = −9<0 → saddle point.
Question 6 of 15
For f(x,y) = x² + y², the point (0,0) is a:
✓ A is correct. fₓ = 2x = 0, fᵧ = 2y = 0 ⟹ (0,0) is critical. Hessian: fₓₓ=2, fᵧᵧ=2, fₓᵧ=0. D = 4 > 0, fₓₓ = 2 > 0 → local minimum. Since f(x,y) = x²+y² ≥ 0 with equality only at (0,0), it's also the global minimum.
Question 7 of 15
The chain rule for z = f(x(t), y(t)) gives dz/dt as:
✓ B is correct. Multivariable chain rule: dz/dt = (∂f/∂x)(dx/dt) + (∂f/∂y)(dy/dt). This generalizes to n variables: if z = f(x₁,…,xₙ) and each xᵢ = xᵢ(t), then dz/dt = Σ (∂f/∂xᵢ)(dxᵢ/dt). In matrix form, this is the product of the gradient row vector with the derivative column vector.
Question 8 of 15
The Inverse Function Theorem guarantees a local C¹ inverse near a when:
✓ C is correct. IvFT: f C¹ and det J_f(a) ≠ 0 ⟹ f is locally C¹-invertible near a. The Jacobian being non-singular (det ≠ 0) is the key condition. Note: this is a LOCAL result — even if det J_f(a) ≠ 0 everywhere, f may not be globally invertible (e.g., z = eˣ on ℝ: J = eˣ ≠ 0 everywhere, but eˣ is not injective from ℂ to ℂ).
Question 9 of 15
For f(x,y) = xy with constraint x² + y² = 1, Lagrange multipliers give maximum value:
✓ B is correct. ∇f = (y,x) = λ(2x,2y) = λ∇g. So y = 2λx and x = 2λy. Multiply: xy = 4λ²xy. If xy≠0: 4λ² = 1, λ = ±1/2. From y = 2λx with λ=1/2: y=x. With x²+y²=1: 2x²=1, x=y=1/√2. f(1/√2,1/√2) = 1/2. Also (x,y) = (−1/√2, −1/√2) gives max = 1/2. Minimum = −1/2 (at x = 1/√2, y = −1/√2).
Question 10 of 15
The second-order test D = fₓₓ fᵧᵧ − fₓᵧ² < 0 at a critical point means:
✓ C is correct. D = det(Hessian) = fₓₓfᵧᵧ − (fₓᵧ)². At a critical point: D<0 → saddle point (indefinite Hessian). D>0 and fₓₓ>0 → local min (positive definite). D>0 and fₓₓ<0 → local max (negative definite). D=0 → inconclusive.
Question 11 of 15
The Jacobian of the spherical coordinate transformation has |det J| equal to:
✓ B is correct. For spherical coordinates: x = ρ sin φ cos θ, y = ρ sin φ sin θ, z = ρ cos φ. The Jacobian determinant is |det J| = ρ² sin φ. This is why ∭ f dV = ∭ f(ρ,φ,θ) ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ. This is one of the most important formulas in multivariable calculus.
Question 12 of 15
If F(x,y) = x² + y² − 1 and (a,b) = (1,0), can we apply IFT to solve for y near (1,0)?
✓ B is correct. ∂F/∂y = 2y. At (1,0): ∂F/∂y = 2(0) = 0. Since this is zero, IFT CANNOT be applied to solve for y near (1,0). Geometrically: at the point (1,0) on the unit circle, the tangent is vertical — y is not a function of x near this point (the circle has two branches: y = ±√(1−x²)). We can however solve for x (∂F/∂x = 2 ≠ 0 at (1,0)).
Question 13 of 15
If f: ℝⁿ → ℝⁿ with det J_f(a) ≠ 0, then near a, f is:
✓ B is correct. Inverse Function Theorem: non-singular Jacobian at a → LOCAL C¹ invertibility near a. It is NOT global invertibility — f might not be injective far from a. Example: f(x) = x³ on ℝ has non-singular derivative everywhere yet is globally injective; but f(x) = x−x³/3 has non-singular derivative at 0 but is not globally injective. IvFT is always a LOCAL statement.
Question 14 of 15
The gradient ∇f at a point a gives:
✓ B is correct. ∇f(a) points in the direction of steepest increase of f at a, and |∇f(a)| is the rate of change in that direction. Directional derivative: Dᵤf(a) = ∇f(a)·u ≤ |∇f(a)| with equality when u = ∇f/|∇f|. The gradient is perpendicular to the level curves/surfaces of f. D is wrong: the level set is where f is constant, and ∇f is perpendicular to it.
Question 15 of 15
Which condition ensures f: ℝ² → ℝ is differentiable at (a,b)?
✓ C is correct. Sufficient condition for differentiability: continuous partial derivatives ⟹ differentiable. A is not enough. B (existence of partials) is not enough — the classic counterexample shows partials can exist without differentiability. D (continuity alone) is not enough. C is the standard sufficient condition taught for multivariable differentiability.

📌 Summary & CSIR NET Tips

Partial Derivatives

  • Partials exist ≠ differentiable
  • Continuous partials ⟹ differentiable
  • Clairaut: fₓᵧ = fᵧₓ when mixed partials continuous
  • Counterexample: f = xy(x²−y²)/(x²+y²) at (0,0)

Jacobian & Chain Rule

  • Jacobian of polar: |J| = r
  • Jacobian of spherical: |J| = ρ² sin φ
  • Chain rule: dz/dt = fₓx'(t) + fᵧy'(t)
  • IvFT: det J ≠ 0 → locally invertible

IFT — Core

  • Solve F(x,y)=0 for y: need ∂F/∂y ≠ 0
  • dy/dx = −(∂F/∂x)/(∂F/∂y)
  • At (1,0) on unit circle: ∂F/∂y = 0 → can't solve for y
  • F C¹ required for IFT conclusion to be C¹

CSIR Exam Tips

  • D = fₓₓfᵧᵧ − fₓᵧ²: negative = saddle
  • Lagrange: ∇f = λ∇g plus constraint
  • IFT condition: ∂F/∂y ≠ 0 at the point
  • Jacobian determinant for change of variables
  • Gradient perpendicular to level curves
