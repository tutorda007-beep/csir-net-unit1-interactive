Unit 1 — Analysis · Topic 07 of 11
Sequences & Series of Functions
The subtle difference between pointwise and uniform convergence — and why it's the dividing line for preserving continuity, differentiability, and integration
CSIR NET High-Yield Function Spaces Advanced Analysis 75 min Study

Learning Objectives

  1. Define pointwise convergence and find the pointwise limit of fₙ(x)
  2. Define uniform convergence and verify it via sup-norm or Cauchy criterion
  3. Show uniform convergence preserves continuity, integration, and differentiation
  4. Apply Weierstrass M-test to series of functions
  5. Understand power series: radius of convergence, uniform convergence on compact subsets
🎯
CSIR NET Frequency: Uniform convergence appears in 2–4 questions per exam. Key questions: "Does fₙ converge uniformly on [0,1]?", "Can we swap limit and integral?", Weierstrass M-test applications, power series radius of convergence.

→ Pointwise Convergence

f
Concept 1
Pointwise Convergence of Functions
Pointwise Convergence
A sequence (fₙ) of functions on D converges pointwise to f if:

∀x ∈ D, ∀ε > 0, ∃N(x,ε) such that n > N ⟹ |fₙ(x) − f(x)| < ε

Key: N may depend on BOTH x AND ε. The limit is taken at each point separately.

Classic Pointwise Limit Examples

Sequence fₙ(x)DomainPointwise Limit f(x)Notes
xⁿ[0,1]0 if 0≤x<1; 1 if x=1f discontinuous; fₙ all continuous
nx·e^{−nx}[0,∞)0Max of fₙ = 1/e at x=1/n → 0 but slowly
x/(1+nx)[0,1]0Pointwise 0; convergence not uniform
sin(nx)/nℝ0Pointwise and uniform convergence (sup ≤ 1/n)
n·x·(1−x)ⁿ[0,1]0Integral of fₙ ≠ integral of limit!
⚡
Pointwise Failures: Pointwise convergence alone does NOT guarantee: (1) f is continuous even if all fₙ are, (2) lim ∫fₙ = ∫f, (3) lim f'ₙ = f'. These require uniform convergence (or stronger conditions).

⟶ Uniform Convergence

UC
Concept 2
Uniform Convergence Definition and Geometry
Uniform Convergence
fₙ converges uniformly to f on D if:

∀ε > 0, ∃N(ε) such that ∀x ∈ D, n > N ⟹ |fₙ(x) − f(x)| < ε

Equivalently: sup_{x∈D} |fₙ(x) − f(x)| → 0 as n → ∞.

Key: N depends ONLY on ε, not on x. The whole graph of fₙ enters the ε-tube around f simultaneously.

Preservation Theorems under Uniform Convergence

Theorem — UC Preserves Continuity
If fₙ → f uniformly on D and each fₙ is continuous, then f is continuous on D.

Converse is false: fₙ(x) = xⁿ on [0,1] — each fₙ continuous but limit f is discontinuous at 1. Convergence is NOT uniform (check: sup|fₙ(x) − f(x)| = 1 for all n).
Theorem — UC Allows Interchange of Limits and Integrals
If fₙ → f uniformly on [a,b], then:

lim_{n→∞} ∫_a^b fₙ(x) dx = ∫_a^b f(x) dx = ∫_a^b lim_{n→∞} fₙ(x) dx
Proof: |∫fₙ − ∫f| ≤ ∫|fₙ − f| ≤ (b−a)·sup|fₙ − f| → 0.
Theorem — UC Allows Interchange of Limits and Derivatives
If fₙ → f pointwise on [a,b], and fₙ are differentiable with f'ₙ → g uniformly on [a,b], then f is differentiable and f' = g:

lim_{n→∞} f'ₙ(x) = (lim_{n→∞} fₙ(x))' = f'(x)
Note: This requires uniform convergence of the DERIVATIVES, not of fₙ themselves.

🧪 Tests for Uniform Convergence

M
Concept 3
Criteria for Uniform Convergence
Sup-Norm Criterion
fₙ → f uniformly on D iff ‖fₙ − f‖_∞ = sup_{x∈D} |fₙ(x) − f(x)| → 0.

Strategy: find the pointwise limit f, compute sup_{x∈D}|fₙ(x) − f(x)|, check if → 0.
Cauchy Criterion for Uniform Convergence
(fₙ) converges uniformly iff: ∀ε > 0, ∃N: m,n > N ⟹ sup_x |fₙ(x) − fₘ(x)| < ε. This doesn't require knowing the limit explicitly.
Dini's Theorem
If (fₙ) is a monotone sequence of continuous functions on a compact set K converging pointwise to a continuous function f, then fₙ → f uniformly on K.
This is remarkable: pointwise convergence of MONOTONE continuous functions to continuous limit on compact set → automatically uniform. Key conditions: all four properties (monotone, continuous, compact domain, continuous limit).

Non-Uniform Convergence Criterion

fₙ does NOT converge uniformly to f on D if: sup_{x∈D}|fₙ(x) − f(x)| ↛ 0, i.e., there exist xₙ ∈ D such that |fₙ(xₙ) − f(xₙ)| ≥ ε₀ > 0.

Non-UC Example
Show fₙ(x) = xⁿ does NOT converge uniformly on [0,1].
Pointwise limit: f(x) = 0 for x∈[0,1) and f(1) = 1.
sup_{x∈[0,1]}|fₙ(x) − f(x)| = sup_{x∈[0,1)}|xⁿ − 0| + |1 − 1| = sup_{x∈[0,1)} xⁿ = 1 (approaching 1 as x→1⁻).
Since sup = 1 ↛ 0, convergence is NOT uniform. □
Alternatively: limit function f is discontinuous but each fₙ is continuous → if convergence were uniform, limit would be continuous (contradiction).

∑ Series of Functions

∑
Concept 4
Weierstrass M-Test and Series Convergence
Series of Functions
Given functions fₙ: D → ℝ, the series ∑fₙ(x) converges uniformly on D if the partial sums Sₙ(x) = f₁(x) + … + fₙ(x) converge uniformly to S(x).
Weierstrass M-Test
If |fₙ(x)| ≤ Mₙ for all x ∈ D and all n, and ∑Mₙ < ∞ (convergent series of constants), then ∑fₙ(x) converges uniformly and absolutely on D.
Proof: The tail sum satisfies |∑_{k=n+1}^∞ fₖ(x)| ≤ ∑_{k=n+1}^∞ Mₖ → 0 (since ∑Mₙ < ∞). This bound is uniform in x.

Weierstrass M-Test Applications

Series ∑fₙ(x)Domain DMₙ boundConclusion
∑ sin(nx)/n²ℝMₙ = 1/n² (∑1/n² = π²/6 <∞)Uniformly convergent on ℝ
∑ xⁿ[−r, r], 0<r<1Mₙ = rⁿ (∑rⁿ = 1/(1−r) <∞)Uniformly convergent on [−r,r]
∑ eˣ/n²[0,1]Mₙ = e/n² (∑ converges)Uniformly convergent on [0,1]
∑ n·xⁿ[0,1)No uniform bound (Mₙ = n at x=1)Pointwise only; NOT uniform on [0,1)
💡
M-Test is sufficient but not necessary: A series can converge uniformly even when the M-test fails. Example: ∑(−1)ⁿ/n converges (alternating series) but there's no Mₙ bound with ∑Mₙ < ∞. Use Dirichlet's test or Abel's test in such cases.

z^n Power Series

R
Concept 5
Power Series and Radius of Convergence
Power Series
A power series centered at x₀ is: ∑_{n=0}^∞ aₙ(x − x₀)ⁿ

Radius of convergence R: The series converges absolutely for |x − x₀| < R and diverges for |x − x₀| > R.
R = 1/limsup |aₙ|^{1/n} = lim |aₙ/aₙ₊₁| (when this limit exists).
Key Power Series Properties
1. A power series converges uniformly on every compact subset [x₀−r, x₀+r] with r < R.
2. The sum function is infinitely differentiable inside the interval of convergence.
3. Term-by-term integration and differentiation is valid inside the interval of convergence.
4. The derived series ∑ naₙ(x−x₀)^{n-1} has the SAME radius of convergence as the original.

Finding Radius of Convergence

Power Series ∑aₙxⁿR (Radius)Method
∑ xⁿR = 1Ratio: |aₙ₊₁/aₙ| = 1
∑ xⁿ/n!R = ∞Ratio: n!/(n+1)! = 1/(n+1) → 0
∑ n!xⁿR = 0Ratio: (n+1) → ∞
∑ xⁿ/nR = 1Root: n^{1/n} → 1
∑ nⁿxⁿR = 0Root: n → ∞
∑ x^{2n}/4ⁿR = 2Substitute: z = x², radius in z is 4, so |x| < 2
Abel's Theorem
If ∑aₙxⁿ has radius of convergence R and ∑aₙRⁿ converges, then lim_{x→R⁻} ∑aₙxⁿ = ∑aₙRⁿ. (The sum function is continuous at the boundary if the series converges there.)
Example: ∑(−1)ⁿ⁺¹xⁿ/n = ln(1+x) for |x|<1. At x=1: ∑(−1)ⁿ⁺¹/n = ln 2 (alternating harmonic). By Abel's theorem, lim_{x→1⁻} ln(1+x) = ln 2 ✓.

✏️ Worked Examples

Example 1 — Uniform Convergence Test
Does fₙ(x) = x/(1+nx) converge uniformly on [0,∞)?
Pointwise limit: For x = 0: fₙ(0) = 0 → 0. For x > 0: fₙ(x) = x/(1+nx) = 1/(1/x + n) → 0 as n→∞. So f(x) = 0.
Sup-norm: sup_{x≥0}|fₙ(x) − 0| = sup_{x≥0} x/(1+nx). Let g(x) = x/(1+nx). g'(x) = (1+nx−nx)/(1+nx)² = 1/(1+nx)² > 0, so g is increasing. As x→∞: g(x) → 1/n.
sup = lim_{x→∞} x/(1+nx) = 1/n → 0. ✓
Therefore fₙ converges uniformly to 0 on [0,∞)!
Contrast: on [0,1], same calculation gives sup ≤ 1/n → 0. ✓ Uniform on both.
Example 2 — Interchange of Limit and Integral
Verify that lim_{n→∞}∫₀¹ nx·e^{−nx²} dx = 0 but ∫₀¹ lim_{n→∞}[nx·e^{−nx²}] dx = 0 too. Check if uniform convergence holds.
fₙ(x) = nx·e^{−nx²}. Pointwise: for x > 0, fₙ(x) → 0 (exponential kills nx). f₀(0) = 0.
∫₀¹ nx·e^{−nx²} dx = [−e^{−nx²}/2]₀¹ = (1/2)(1 − e^{−n}) → 1/2 as n → ∞.
But ∫₀¹ f(x) dx = ∫₀¹ 0 dx = 0. So 1/2 ≠ 0!
Conclusion: Limit and integral cannot be swapped → convergence is NOT uniform on [0,1]. Check: sup_{x∈[0,1]} fₙ(x): maximum at x = 1/√(2n) with value √(n/(2e)) → ∞. So sup → ∞ ≠ 0 — confirming non-uniform convergence.
Example 3 — M-Test Application
Show that f(x) = ∑_{n=1}^∞ sin(nx)/n² is continuous on ℝ.
|sin(nx)/n²| ≤ 1/n² = Mₙ for all x ∈ ℝ.
∑Mₙ = ∑1/n² = π²/6 < ∞.
By Weierstrass M-test, ∑sin(nx)/n² converges uniformly on ℝ.
Each partial sum is continuous (finite sum of continuous functions). By UC theorem, f is continuous on ℝ. □
(Furthermore, term-by-term differentiation: f'(x) = ∑cos(nx)/n — this series needs separate verification of uniform convergence.)
Example 4 — Power Series Radius
Find the radius of convergence and interval of convergence of ∑_{n=1}^∞ (−1)ⁿ x²ⁿ/n.
Let z = x². Series becomes ∑(−1)ⁿ zⁿ/n. Ratio test: |z|·(n/(n+1)) → |z|. Converges for |z| < 1, i.e., x² < 1, i.e., |x| < 1. Radius of convergence R = 1.
At x = 1 (z = 1): ∑(−1)ⁿ/n = −ln 2 (alternating harmonic). Converges.
At x = −1 (z = 1): same as above. Converges.
Interval of convergence: [−1, 1].
Example 5 — Dini's Theorem Application
Let fₙ(x) = 1/(1+xⁿ) on [0,1]. Find the pointwise limit. Is convergence uniform?
Pointwise: For x ∈ [0,1): xⁿ → 0, so fₙ(x) → 1/(1+0) = 1. For x = 1: fₙ(1) = 1/2. For x > 1 (if domain extended): fₙ(x) → 0.
On [0,1]: limit f(x) = 1 for x∈[0,1), f(1) = 1/2.
f is discontinuous at x = 1. Since each fₙ is continuous but limit is not, convergence is NOT uniform on [0,1].
However, on [0, r] for r < 1: fₙ → 1 uniformly (sup|fₙ(x)−1| = sup xⁿ/(1+xⁿ) ≤ rⁿ/(1−rⁿ) → 0).

Question 5 of 15

✓ B is correct. This is a direct application of the Weierstrass M-test. Mₙ = 1/n², and ∑1/n² = π²/6 < ∞. Since |sin(nx)/n²| ≤ 1/n² for all x, the M-test gives uniform and absolute convergence on all of ℝ.
Question 8 of 15
Dini's Theorem guarantees uniform convergence when:
✓ B is correct. Dini's Theorem: monotone sequence of continuous functions on compact set K, with continuous pointwise limit → convergence is automatically uniform. All four conditions are necessary: (1) monotone, (2) each fₙ continuous, (3) compact domain, (4) limit function continuous. Drop any one and Dini fails.
Question 9 of 15
The radius of convergence of ∑ n!xⁿ is:
✓ A is correct. Ratio test: |aₙ₊₁/aₙ| = (n+1)!/n! = n+1 → ∞. So 1/R = ∞, meaning R = 0. The series only converges at x = 0. This is the opposite extreme from ∑xⁿ/n! (R=∞). Factorial growth of coefficients → R = 0.
Question 10 of 15
For uniform convergence of fₙ to f on D, which does NOT hold?
✓ D is NOT guaranteed. Uniform convergence of fₙ does NOT automatically allow term-by-term differentiation. For that, you need uniform convergence of the DERIVATIVES f'ₙ (not fₙ). Example: fₙ(x) = sin(nx)/n → 0 uniformly, but f'ₙ(x) = cos(nx) does not converge. A, B, C all follow from uniform convergence.
Question 11 of 15
The power series ∑ xⁿ/n converges at x = −1 but diverges at x = 1. By Abel's theorem:
✓ A is correct. ∑xⁿ/n = −ln(1−x) for |x| < 1. At x = −1: ∑(−1)ⁿ/n = −ln 2 (alternating harmonic series). By Abel's theorem, since the series converges at x = −1, the sum is continuous there: lim_{x→(−1)⁺} (−ln(1−x)) = −ln(2) = ∑(−1)ⁿ/n. At x=1, diverges (harmonic series), confirming lim = ∞ is approached.
Question 12 of 15
If fₙ → f pointwise and the convergence is NOT uniform, which can still hold?
✓ B is correct. Without uniform convergence, there's no guarantee about interchanging limits with integrals or derivatives. But it MIGHT still hold for specific examples. A is wrong (limit can be discontinuous). C is wrong (fails without UC of derivatives). D is wrong (fails without additional conditions). Question 13 of 15
The series ∑ 1/(n² + x²) converges uniformly on ℝ because:
✓ B is correct. n² + x² ≥ n² for all x ∈ ℝ, so 1/(n²+x²) ≤ 1/n². Since ∑1/n² = π²/6 < ∞, M-test applies: ∑1/(n²+x²) converges uniformly and absolutely on ℝ. The sum is a continuous function of x (uniform limit of continuous functions).
Question 14 of 15
A power series ∑aₙxⁿ with radius R > 0 converges uniformly on:
✓ B is correct. A power series converges uniformly on every compact (closed bounded) subinterval [−r, r] with r < R. On the open interval (−R, R), convergence is absolute but may not be uniform (the supremum could be attained in the limit as x → R). At the endpoints ±R, convergence must be checked separately (can converge or diverge).

📌 Summary & CSIR NET Tips

Pointwise vs Uniform

  • Pointwise: N depends on x and ε
  • Uniform: N depends only on ε (same for all x)
  • UC ⟹ Pointwise (not converse)
  • UC ⟺ ‖fₙ−f‖_∞ → 0
  • fₙ = xⁿ on [0,1]: pointwise but NOT uniform

Preservation Theorems

  • UC + continuous fₙ ⟹ continuous f
  • UC ⟹ lim∫fₙ = ∫limfₙ
  • Uniform conv. of f'ₙ (+ ptwise fₙ) ⟹ (limfₙ)' = limf'ₙ
  • Dini: monotone + cts + compact + cts limit ⟹ UC

Series of Functions

  • M-test: |fₙ|≤Mₙ, ∑Mₙ<∞ ⟹ uniform + absolute conv.
  • ∑sin(nx)/n²: UC on ℝ (Mₙ=1/n²)
  • Power series: UC on compact subsets of (−R,R)
  • Derived series has same radius R
  • Abel's theorem: continuity at boundary if convergent

CSIR Exam Tips

  • Check sup|fₙ−f| → 0 to verify UC
  • Discontinuous limit ⟹ convergence NOT uniform
  • Radius of convergence: ratio test or root test
  • ∑1/n²: use for M-test bound
  • Exchange lim and integral needs UC (or Lebesgue DCT)
