Introduction

A PDE involves a function of several variables and its partial derivatives. The classification of second-order PDEs (elliptic/parabolic/hyperbolic) determines which initial/boundary conditions are appropriate and what qualitative behavior solutions exhibit.

Order & Linearity

General PDE

F(x, y, u, u_x, u_y, u_xx, u_xy, u_yy, …) = 0. The order is the highest partial derivative.

TypeDefinitionExamples
LinearLinear in u and all its derivatives (coefficients may depend on x,y)u_xx+u_yy=0 (Laplace), u_t=ku_xx (Heat)
Semi-linearLinear in highest-order derivatives; nonlinear lower termsu_xx+u_yy=f(x,y,u)
Quasi-linearLinear in highest-order derivatives; coefficients depend on lower-order termsuu_x+u_y=0
Fully nonlinearNonlinear in highest-order derivativesu_x²+u_y²=1 (Eikonal)

Second-Order Classification

General 2nd-Order PDE (2 variables)
Au_xx + Bu_xy + Cu_yy + Du_x + Eu_y + Fu = G

where A, B, C may depend on x, y (quasi-linear classification done pointwise).

Discriminant Rule
Δ = B² − 4AC Δ < 0 ⟹ Elliptic (e.g., Laplace equation) Δ = 0 ⟹ Parabolic (e.g., Heat equation) Δ > 0 ⟹ Hyperbolic (e.g., Wave equation)
TypeDiscriminantPrototypePhysical Meaning
EllipticB²−4AC < 0u_xx+u_yy=0 (Laplace)Steady-state, equilibrium
ParabolicB²−4AC = 0u_t=u_xx (Heat)Diffusion, irreversible
HyperbolicB²−4AC > 0u_tt=c²u_xx (Wave)Wave propagation, reversible
Examples
  • u_xx+u_yy=0: A=1,B=0,C=1 ⟹ Δ=0−4=−4 <0 → Elliptic
  • u_t=u_xx: A=1,B=0,C=0 ⟹ Δ=0 → Parabolic
  • u_tt=u_xx: A=1,B=0,C=−1 ⟹ Δ=0+4=4>0 → Hyperbolic
  • u_xx+u_yy+u_zz=0 (3D Laplace): elliptic

Canonical Forms

Change of Variables to Canonical Form

New coordinates ξ=ξ(x,y), η=η(x,y) chosen so that the leading-order part simplifies.

TypeCanonical FormNote
Ellipticu_ξξ+u_ηη = lower orderξ,η are complex conjugate characteristics
Parabolicu_ξξ = lower orderOne characteristic family
Hyperbolicu_ξη = lower order (first canonical form)Two real characteristic families
Hyperbolicu_αα−u_ββ = lower order (second canonical form)α=ξ+η, β=ξ−η
Characteristics

Characteristic curves satisfy A(dy)²−B(dx)(dy)+C(dx)²=0. For hyperbolic: two real families; parabolic: one (repeated); elliptic: none (complex).

dy/dx = [B ± √(B²−4AC)] / (2A)

Well-Posedness (Hadamard)

Hadamard Well-Posedness

A problem is well-posed if:

  1. Existence: a solution exists.
  2. Uniqueness: the solution is unique.
  3. Stability: the solution depends continuously on data.
PDE TypeAppropriate ConditionsIll-posed if…
EllipticBoundary conditions (Dirichlet, Neumann, Robin) on closed domainInitial conditions prescribed
ParabolicInitial condition + boundary conditionsBackward heat equation (ill-posed)
HyperbolicInitial conditions (Cauchy problem), + BCsOver/under-determined BCs
Hadamard's Example (Ill-posed Elliptic Cauchy Problem)

Laplace equation Δu=0 with u(x,0)=0, u_y(x,0)=sin(nx)/n. Solution: u=sin(nx)sinh(ny)/n². For large n: data→0 but solution grows exponentially → NOT stable → ill-posed.

Summary

Discriminant

Δ=B²−4AC. Δ<0: elliptic; Δ=0: parabolic; Δ>0: hyperbolic.

Elliptic

Laplace u_xx+u_yy=0. Steady-state. Needs BCs. No real characteristics.

Parabolic

Heat u_t=ku_xx. Diffusion. ICs+BCs. One characteristic family.

Hyperbolic

Wave u_tt=c²u_xx. Propagation. Cauchy ICs. Two real characteristic families.

Canonical Forms

Transforms to u_ξξ+u_ηη (elliptic), u_ξξ (parabolic), u_ξη (hyperbolic).

Well-Posedness

Existence + Uniqueness + Continuous dependence. Backward heat = ill-posed.
