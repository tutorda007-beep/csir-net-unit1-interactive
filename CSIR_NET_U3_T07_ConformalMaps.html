Introduction

Conformal mappings are angle-preserving holomorphic functions. They provide a powerful geometric viewpoint on complex analysis and are essential tools in applications: solving Laplace's equation, fluid dynamics, electrostatics, and more. Möbius transformations form the automorphism group of the Riemann sphere.

Conformal Mappings

Definition

A map f is conformal (angle-preserving) at z₀ if it preserves angles between curves through z₀, including their orientation. A holomorphic function f is conformal at z₀ if and only if f'(z₀) ≠ 0.

Conformal ↔ Holomorphic with nonzero derivative

f is conformal at z₀ ⟺ f is holomorphic at z₀ and f'(z₀) ≠ 0.

The local behavior of f near z₀: rotation by arg(f'(z₀)) and scaling by |f'(z₀)|.

Critical Points

At a zero of order k of f', the map multiplies angles by (k+1). So at a critical point (f'(z₀)=0 of order k), angles are multiplied by k+1 — the map is NOT conformal there.

Example

f(z)=z² is conformal everywhere except z=0. At z=0, f'(0)=0 (critical point of order 1), and angles are doubled (k+1=2). A right angle becomes a straight angle.

Möbius Transformations

Möbius (Linear Fractional) Transformation
w = f(z) = (az + b)/(cz + d), where ad − bc ≠ 0

These are conformal bijections of the Riemann sphere ℂ̂ = ℂ ∪ {∞}. The condition ad−bc ≠ 0 ensures f is non-degenerate.

Properties of Möbius Transformations

  • Group structure: Composition of two Möbius transforms is Möbius. They form a group (PSL(2,ℂ)).
  • Circle-preserving: Every Möbius transformation maps circles/lines to circles/lines (on ℂ̂, lines are circles through ∞).
  • Three-point determined: A Möbius transformation is uniquely determined by its values at any three distinct points.
  • Fixed points: Has at most 2 fixed points in ℂ̂ (unless it's the identity).
  • Inverse: f⁻¹(w) = (dw−b)/(−cw+a)

Cross-Ratio

Cross-Ratio
(z, z₁; z₂, z₃) = (z−z₂)(z₁−z₃) / [(z−z₃)(z₁−z₂)]

The cross-ratio is preserved by Möbius transformations. To find the Möbius map sending z₁↦w₁, z₂↦w₂, z₃↦w₃: set (z, z₁; z₂, z₃) = (w, w₁; w₂, w₃) and solve for w.

Disk Automorphisms

All conformal automorphisms of the unit disk D are Möbius transforms of the form:

f(z) = e^(iθ) · (z − a)/(1 − āz), |a| < 1, θ ∈ ℝ

These are the Blaschke factors.

Schwarz-Christoffel Formula

Schwarz-Christoffel

Maps the upper half-plane to a polygon with interior angles α₁π, α₂π, ..., αₙπ:

f(z) = A ∫₀ᶻ (t−x₁)^(α₁−1)···(t−xₙ)^(αₙ−1) dt + B

where x₁ < x₂ < ··· < xₙ are real preimages of the vertices.

Riemann Mapping Theorem

Riemann Mapping Theorem

Every simply connected proper open subset of ℂ is conformally equivalent to the open unit disk D.

Precisely: if U ⊊ ℂ is simply connected and open, and z₀ ∈ U, then there exists a unique conformal bijection f: U → D such that f(z₀)=0 and f'(z₀)>0 (positive real).

Exceptions

The full plane ℂ itself and the Riemann sphere ℂ̂ are NOT conformally equivalent to D (by Liouville's theorem, a bounded entire function is constant, so D→ℂ bijectively is impossible).

Consequence for Laplace's Equation

If f:D→U is conformal, and φ satisfies Laplace's equation Δφ=0 on U (a boundary value problem), then φ∘f satisfies Δ(φ∘f)=0 on D. This reduces the problem to solving Laplace's equation on a disk — the standard domain.
