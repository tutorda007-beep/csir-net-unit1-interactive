Introduction

A function f: ℂ → ℂ is analytic (holomorphic) at z₀ if it is complex-differentiable in a neighborhood of z₀. This single condition (differentiable in ℂ) imposes two PDE constraints (CR equations) on the real and imaginary parts, making analytic functions enormously rigid and well-behaved.

Complex Differentiability

Complex Derivative

f: Ω → ℂ (Ω open) is complex differentiable at z₀ ∈ Ω if the limit:

f'(z₀) = lim_{z→z₀} (f(z) − f(z₀))/(z − z₀)

exists (as z → z₀ from ANY direction in ℂ). This is a much stronger condition than real differentiability.

Holomorphic / Analytic
  • Holomorphic at z₀: complex differentiable in an open neighbourhood of z₀
  • Holomorphic on Ω: holomorphic at every point of Ω
  • Entire: holomorphic on all of ℂ (e.g., polynomials, eᶻ, sin z, cos z)

Differentiation Rules

All standard calculus rules hold: sum, product, quotient, chain rule — with complex variables.

Examples

f(z) = zⁿ: f'(z) = nzⁿ⁻¹. f(z) = eᶻ: f'(z) = eᶻ. f(z) = sin z: f'(z) = cos z.

f(z) = z̄ (conjugate): NOT differentiable anywhere in ℂ (fails CR). f(z) = |z|²: differentiable only at z = 0.

Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Write f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y) where z = x + iy. Then f is holomorphic iff u and v have continuous partial derivatives satisfying:

∂u/∂x = ∂v/∂y and ∂u/∂y = −∂v/∂x

(The 2nd pair can be written: u_x = v_y and u_y = −v_x.)

When these hold and the partials are continuous, f'(z) = u_x + iv_x = v_y − iu_y.

CR Equations in Polar Form (r,θ)
r·u_r = v_θ and v_r = −(1/r)u_θ

Useful when the function is expressed in polar coordinates.

Harmonic Functions

Harmonic Functions

h: Ω → ℝ is harmonic if it satisfies Laplace's equation: Δh = h_xx + h_yy = 0.

If f = u + iv is holomorphic, then BOTH u and v are harmonic (consequence of CR equations).

Harmonic Conjugate

Given harmonic u on a simply connected domain Ω, there exists a harmonic function v such that f = u + iv is holomorphic. Such v is called the harmonic conjugate of u (determined up to a constant).

Construction: v_x = −u_y and v_y = u_x (from CR equations). Integrate along a path.

Example: Find harmonic conjugate of u = x² − y²

v_y = u_x = 2x, so v = 2xy + g(x). Then v_x = 2y + g'(x) = −u_y = 2y, so g'(x) = 0, g = const. Thus v = 2xy + C.

Check: f = (x²−y²) + 2xyi = (x+iy)² = z². ✓

Key Identity: |eⁱᶻ| ≠ 1 in general

For z = x+iy: |eⁱᶻ| = |e^(ix−y)| = e^(−y). So |eⁱᶻ|=1 only when y=Im(z)=0 (real z). For complex z, eⁱᶻ is not on the unit circle in general.

Singularities — Preview

Types of Isolated Singularities

A function f has an isolated singularity at z₀ if f is holomorphic on a punctured disc 0 < |z−z₀| < r.

  • Removable singularity: lim_{z→z₀} f(z) exists and is finite. f extends to an analytic function at z₀.
  • Pole of order m: lim_{z→z₀} (z−z₀)^m f(z) exists ≠ 0. Laurent series has finitely many negative powers.
  • Essential singularity: Neither removable nor pole. Laurent series has infinitely many negative-power terms. Casorati-Weierstrass: f(D\{z₀}) is dense in ℂ.
Riemann's Removable Singularity Theorem

If f is holomorphic on D\{z₀} and bounded near z₀ (|f(z)|≤M for z near z₀), then z₀ is a removable singularity.
