Introduction

Rings generalize familiar number systems: ℤ, ℚ, ℝ, ℂ are all rings. Rings have two operations (addition and multiplication). Ideals play the role in rings that normal subgroups play in groups — they allow us to form quotient rings. The structure theory of rings (PID, UFD, fields) underlies much of modern algebra and number theory.

Rings: Basic Definitions

Ring

A ring (R, +, ·) is a set with two operations satisfying:

  1. (R, +) is an abelian group (with identity 0)
  2. (R, ·) is associative
  3. Distributivity: a(b+c) = ab+ac and (b+c)a = ba+ca

If also 1∈R with 1·a=a·1=a: ring with unity. If ab=ba for all a,b: commutative ring.

Units and Zero Divisors

Unit: a∈R with a multiplicative inverse a⁻¹∈R. Group of units = R*.

Zero divisor: nonzero a∈R with ab=0 or ba=0 for some nonzero b.

In ℤ₆: 2·3=6≡0, so 2 and 3 are zero divisors. Units of ℤ₆: {1,5}.

Ideals

Ideal

A subset I ⊆ R is an ideal if:

  1. (I, +) is a subgroup of (R, +)
  2. For all r∈R and a∈I: ra ∈ I and ar ∈ I

Left ideal: only ra∈I. Right ideal: only ar∈I. Two-sided ideal: both.

Principal Ideals

Principal Ideal

The ideal generated by element a: (a) = {ra : r∈R} (in commutative ring with unity).

In ℤ: every ideal is principal — (n) = nℤ = {multiples of n}.

Special Ideals

Prime and Maximal Ideals

Prime ideal P: if ab∈P then a∈P or b∈P. (R/P is an integral domain.)

Maximal ideal M: no ideal strictly between M and R. (R/M is a field.)

Every maximal ideal is prime. In PID, prime ⟺ maximal (for proper ideals).

Examples in ℤ
  • (p) = pℤ is prime ⟺ p is prime integer. Also maximal in ℤ since ℤ is PID.
  • (0) is prime (since ℤ is an integral domain) but not maximal.
  • (6) is not prime: 2·3∈(6) but 2∉(6) and 3∉(6).

Quotient Rings

Quotient Ring R/I

For ideal I ⊲ R: R/I = {a+I : a∈R} with operations:

(a+I) + (b+I) = (a+b)+I, (a+I)(b+I) = ab+I

This is well-defined because I is an ideal (not just a subgroup).

Key Examples
  • ℤ/(n) ≅ ℤₙ.
  • ℝ[x]/(x²+1) ≅ ℂ (complex numbers!).
  • ℤ/(p) ≅ 𝔽ₚ (finite field, p prime).
  • ℤ[x]/(x²−2) ≅ ℤ[√2].
Chinese Remainder Theorem (Ring version)

If I, J are ideals with I+J=R (coprime ideals), then:

R/(I∩J) ≅ R/I × R/J

For ℤ: if gcd(m,n)=1, then ℤ/mnℤ ≅ ℤ/mℤ × ℤ/nℤ.

Ring Homomorphisms

Ring Homomorphism

φ: R → S is a ring homomorphism if:

  • φ(a+b) = φ(a)+φ(b)
  • φ(ab) = φ(a)φ(b)
  • φ(1_R) = 1_S (if rings have unity)

ker φ = {r∈R: φ(r)=0} is an ideal of R. im φ is a subring of S.

First Isomorphism Theorem (Rings)
R/ker φ ≅ im φ

Exact parallel with the group version.

Chain of Inclusions
Fields ⊊ Euclidean Domains ⊊ PIDs ⊊ UFDs ⊊ Integral Domains ⊊ Commutative Rings

ℤ[x] is a UFD but NOT a PID (ideal (2,x) is not principal). ℤ[√−5] is an integral domain but NOT a UFD.

Key Theorem

In a PID: prime ⟺ irreducible ⟺ (p) is a maximal ideal. Every PID is a UFD.

Summary

R

Ring

Two operations: abelian group under +, associative ·, distributive. Units R*, zero divisors.

I⊲R

Ideal

Closed under +, and rI⊆I for all r∈R. (a)=principal ideal.

R/I

Quotient Ring

P prime ⟹ R/P integral domain. M maximal ⟹ R/M field.

CRT

Chinese Remainder

gcd(m,n)=1 ⟹ ℤ/mnℤ≅ℤ/mℤ × ℤ/nℤ.

⊊

Hierarchy

Fields ⊊ Eucl. Domains ⊊ PIDs ⊊ UFDs ⊊ Integral Domains.
