Introduction

Two fundamental constructions in topology: the product topology (build spaces from factors — the coarsest topology making projections continuous) and the quotient topology (build spaces by gluing — the finest topology making the quotient map continuous). Many classical spaces (torus, Möbius band, Klein bottle, projective plane) arise as quotients.

Product Topology

Product Topology (Finite)

On X×Y, the product topology has as basis the sets U×V where U is open in X and V is open in Y.

It is the coarsest topology on X×Y making both projections π₁:X×Y→X and π₂:X×Y→Y continuous.

Product Topology (Arbitrary)

On ∏_α Xα, the Tychonoff product topology has as a sub-basis the sets π_α⁻¹(Uα) for open Uα⊆Xα.

Basis: finite intersections of sub-basis elements (so only finitely many coordinates are restricted).

This is the coarsest topology making all projections πα continuous.

Box vs Product Topology

For infinite products, the box topology takes all ∏Uα (one open set per factor) as a basis. The box topology is strictly finer. The box topology loses many nice properties (e.g., Tychonoff fails). For countable products of ℝ: ℝ^ω under box topology is NOT metrizable by the standard diagonal argument.

Quotient Topology

Quotient Map / Quotient Topology

Given f: X→Y surjective, the quotient topology on Y is: V⊆Y is open iff f⁻¹(V) is open in X.

It is the finest topology on Y making f continuous.

If f comes from an equivalence relation ∼ on X, then Y = X/∼ (equivalence classes) and f: x ↦ [x].

Universal Property of Quotient

g: X/∼ → Z is continuous iff g∘f: X→Z is continuous.

Equivalently: continuous maps on X/∼ correspond to continuous maps on X that are constant on equivalence classes.

Quotient Map ≠ Open Map

A quotient map need not be open (images of open sets may not be open). But: a continuous surjection from a compact space to a Hausdorff space is always a quotient map.

Torus as Product

The torus T²=S¹×S¹ is both a quotient of [0,1]² (identifying edges) and a product of two circles. Key properties: compact (product of compact), connected (product of connected), T₂ (product of Hausdorff).

Tychonoff's Theorem

Tychonoff's Theorem

An arbitrary product of compact topological spaces is compact (in the product topology).

∏_{α∈I} Xα compact ⟺ each Xα is compact

This is one of the most important theorems in topology. It is equivalent to the Axiom of Choice.

Key Application

[0,1]^I is compact for any index set I (by Tychonoff, since [0,1] is compact). The Hilbert cube [0,1]^ℕ is compact. Every compact metrizable space embeds in [0,1]^ℕ (Urysohn metrization + separability).

Banach-Alaoglu Theorem

The closed unit ball of the dual X* of a Banach space X is compact in the weak* topology. Proof: the weak* topology is a subspace topology from a product of intervals, so Tychonoff applies.

Čech-Stone Compactification

Every completely regular (Tychonoff) space X has a compactification βX: a compact Hausdorff space containing X as a dense subspace, universal for continuous maps from X to compact Hausdorff spaces.
