Definitions & Examples

Normed Linear Space Basic

A norm on vector space X is ‖·‖: X→ℝ satisfying: 1. ‖x‖ ≥ 0; ‖x‖=0 ↔ x=0 2. ‖αx‖ = |α|‖x‖ (homogeneity) 3. ‖x+y‖ ≤ ‖x‖+‖y‖ (triangle inequality) (X,‖·‖) is a normed linear space. Key examples: lᵖ = {(xₙ): Σ|xₙ|ᵖ<∞}, ‖x‖ₚ=(Σ|xₙ|ᵖ)^{1/p} l∞ = {(xₙ): sup|xₙ|<∞}, ‖x‖∞=sup|xₙ| C[a,b]: ‖f‖∞=max|f(x)| Lᵖ[a,b]: ‖f‖ₚ=(∫|f|ᵖ)^{1/p} ℝⁿ with ‖x‖ₚ=(Σ|xᵢ|ᵖ)^{1/p}

Banach Spaces Intermediate

A Banach space is a complete normed space: every Cauchy sequence converges. Is it Banach? lᵖ (1≤p≤∞): YES (Banach) L²[a,b]: YES (Banach, also Hilbert) C[a,b] with ‖·‖∞: YES C[a,b] with ‖·‖₁: NO (not complete) c₀₀ (finite seq.) with l² norm: NO Polynomials on [0,1]: NO Riesz's Theorem: A normed space X is finite-dimensional ⟺ its unit ball is compact. Equivalent norms: on finite-dim space, all norms are equivalent. FALSE in infinite dim.

Hölder's InequalityMedium
Hölder's inequality states: if 1/p+1/q=1, then Σ|xₙyₙ| ≤
A‖x‖ₚ + ‖y‖_q
B‖x‖ₚ · ‖y‖_q
C‖x‖ₚ² + ‖y‖_q²
D(‖x‖ₚ + ‖y‖_q)/2
✓ Correct Answer: (B) — ‖x‖ₚ · ‖y‖_q
Hölder's inequality: Σ|xₙyₙ| ≤ ‖x‖ₚ·‖y‖_q when 1/p+1/q=1 (p,q are conjugate exponents). Special case p=q=2: Cauchy-Schwarz: Σ|xₙyₙ|≤‖x‖₂·‖y‖₂. Continuous version: ∫|f·g|≤‖f‖_p·‖g‖_q. Used to prove Minkowski's inequality (triangle ineq for lᵖ norms). Note: conjugate of p=1 is q=∞; Hölder with (1,∞): Σ|xₙyₙ|≤‖x‖₁·‖y‖∞ = Σ|xₙ|·sup|yₙ|.

Norm EquivalenceMedium
On ℝⁿ, which statement about norms ‖·‖ₚ and ‖·‖_q (p≠q) is TRUE?
AThey are not equivalent
BThey are equivalent (induce same topology)
C‖·‖₁ is always smaller than ‖·‖₂
DThey are equivalent only if p,q ≥ 2
✓ Correct Answer: (B) — They are equivalent
On finite-dimensional space ℝⁿ, ALL norms are equivalent: there exist constants 0<c≤C such that c‖x‖_q ≤ ‖x‖_p ≤ C‖x‖_q for all x. Specific bounds in ℝⁿ: ‖x‖₂≤‖x‖₁≤√n‖x‖₂; ‖x‖∞≤‖x‖₂≤√n‖x‖∞. This fails in infinite dimensions: on l², the l¹ norm and l² norm are NOT equivalent (consider eₙ: ‖eₙ‖₂=1 always, but ‖eₙ‖∞=1 and ‖eₙ‖₁=1 too — bad example; consider xₙ=Σ_{k=1}^n 1/k eₖ).

Riesz LemmaAdvanced
Riesz's Lemma states: for a closed proper subspace Y of normed space X, there exists xₑ with ‖xₑ‖=1 and dist(xₑ,Y) ≥ θ for any θ∈(0,1). This is used to show:
AEvery normed space has an orthonormal basis
BThe unit ball of an infinite-dimensional space is not compact
CEvery Cauchy sequence converges
DAll norms on a Banach space are equivalent
✓ Correct Answer: (B) — Unit ball of infinite-dimensional space is not compact
Riesz Lemma is used to show: in an infinite-dimensional normed space, the closed unit ball is NOT compact. Construct sequence x₁, x₂, ... inductively: x₁ arbitrary, xₙ₊₁ chosen so ‖xₙ₊₁‖=1 and dist(xₙ₊₁, span{x₁,...,xₙ})≥1/2 (by Riesz). Then ‖xₙ−xₘ‖≥1/2 for n≠m — no convergent subsequence. Contrast: in finite dimensions, closed bounded sets are compact (Heine-Borel). So compactness of unit ball ⟺ finite-dimensional.

Inner Product Spaces & Hilbert Spaces

Inner Product & Hilbert Space Basic

Inner product ⟨·,·⟩: H×H→ℂ satisfies: 1. ⟨x,x⟩ ≥ 0; ⟨x,x⟩=0 ↔ x=0 2. ⟨αx+βy,z⟩ = α⟨x,z⟩+β⟨y,z⟩ (linear in 1st) 3. ⟨x,y⟩ = ⟨y,x⟩* (conjugate symmetry) Induced norm: ‖x‖ = √⟨x,x⟩ Cauchy-Schwarz: |⟨x,y⟩| ≤ ‖x‖·‖y‖ Parallelogram law: ‖x+y‖²+‖x−y‖²=2(‖x‖²+‖y‖²) Hilbert space = complete inner product space. Examples: ℝⁿ, l², L²[a,b], H^k (Sobolev)

Orthonormality & Gram-Schmidt Intermediate

Orthonormal: {eₙ} with ⟨eₘ,eₙ⟩=δₘₙ Gram-Schmidt from {v₁,...,vₙ}: u₁ = v₁/‖v₁‖ u₂ = (v₂−⟨v₂,u₁⟩u₁)/‖...‖ uₖ = (vₖ − Σⱼ₌₁^{k-1}⟨vₖ,uⱼ⟩uⱼ)/‖...‖ Bessel's inequality: Σ|⟨x,eₙ⟩|² ≤ ‖x‖² Parseval's identity (complete ONB): Σ|⟨x,eₙ⟩|² = ‖x‖² (equality holds) x = Σ ⟨x,eₙ⟩ eₙ (Fourier expansion) Every separable Hilbert space has a countable ONB.

Projection Theorem & Orthogonal Complement Intermediate

Projection Theorem: if M is a closed subspace of Hilbert space H, then every x∈H has unique decomposition: x = m + m⊥ where m∈M (closest point) and m⊥∈M⊥. Best approximation: ‖x−m‖ = dist(x,M) = min_{y∈M}‖x−y‖ (m is the orthogonal projection of x onto M) M⊥ = {y: ⟨y,x⟩=0 ∀x∈M} (orthogonal complement) (M⊥)⊥ = M̄ (closure of M) H = M ⊕ M⊥ (orthogonal direct sum)

Riesz Representation Theorem Advanced

Every bounded linear functional f:H→ℂ on a Hilbert space H is of the form: f(x) = ⟨x,y⟩ for a unique y∈H and ‖f‖ = ‖y‖. H* ≅ H (Hilbert spaces are self-dual) Consequence: Hilbert spaces are reflexive. Self-adjoint (Hermitian) operator: ⟨Tx,y⟩=⟨x,Ty⟩ Eigenvalues are real. Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal. Spectral Theorem (compact self-adjoint T): H = ⊕ Eₖ (eigenspaces), Tx = Σ λₖ⟨x,eₖ⟩eₖ

If T is a bounded self-adjoint operator on a Hilbert space, then its spectrum σ(T) is:
AA subset of the imaginary axis
BA subset of the real line
CThe entire complex plane
DA finite set
✓ Correct Answer: (B) — A subset of the real line

Bounded Linear Operators

Bounded Linear Operators Basic

T:X→Y is bounded ↔ ∃M: ‖Tx‖≤M‖x‖ ∀x ↔ T is continuous ↔ T is continuous at 0 Operator norm: ‖T‖ = sup_{‖x‖=1} ‖Tx‖ = sup_{x≠0} ‖Tx‖/‖x‖ = inf{M: ‖Tx‖≤M‖x‖} B(X,Y) = space of bounded linear operators X→Y with ‖T‖ is a normed space. B(X,Y) is Banach if Y is Banach. B(X,X) = B(X): Banach algebra under composition. ‖ST‖ ≤ ‖S‖·‖T‖ (submultiplicative norm)

Dual Space X* Intermediate

X* = B(X,ℝ) (or B(X,ℂ)): dual space = bounded linear functionals on X. Dual pairs (X, X*): (lᵖ)* ≅ l^q (1/p+1/q=1, 1<p<∞) (l¹)* ≅ l∞ (l∞)* ⊋ l¹ (strictly larger!) (Lᵖ)* ≅ L^q (1≤p<∞) (C[a,b])* = space of Borel measures (Riesz) Bidual X** = (X*)* Natural embedding J:X→X**, J(x)(f)=f(x) Reflexive: J is surjective (X≅X**) Hilbert spaces: X≅X* ≅ X** (self-dual)

Hahn-BanachMedium
The Hahn-Banach theorem guarantees that for any x₀≠0 in a normed space X, there exists f∈X* with:
Af(x₀)=0 and ‖f‖=1
Bf(x₀)=‖x₀‖ and ‖f‖=1
Cf(x₀)=1 and ‖f‖=∞
Df is surjective
✓ Correct Answer: (B) — f(x₀)=‖x₀‖ and ‖f‖=1
By Hahn-Banach (norming functional): for any x₀≠0, there exists f∈X* with f(x₀)=‖x₀‖ and ‖f‖=1. This means X* separates points of X (if f(x)=0 for all f∈X* then x=0). It also gives: ‖x₀‖=sup{|f(x₀)|: f∈X*, ‖f‖≤1}. Consequence: X embeds isometrically into X** via J(x)(f)=f(x), with ‖J(x)‖=‖x‖.

Open Mapping TheoremMedium
If T:X→Y is a bounded bijective linear operator between Banach spaces, then T⁻¹ is:
AUnbounded in general
BBounded (by the open mapping theorem)
COnly bounded if T is compact
DNot necessarily linear
✓ Correct Answer: (B) — Bounded (by the open mapping theorem)
Open Mapping Theorem → Bounded Inverse Theorem: if T:X→Y is a surjective bounded linear operator between Banach spaces, T maps open sets to open sets. If additionally T is injective (bijective), then T⁻¹:Y→X is bounded. Proof: T⁻¹ is linear. T⁻¹ is continuous ↔ T⁻¹ maps open sets to open sets ↔ T maps open sets to open sets ✓ (by OMT). This fails for incomplete spaces: even a bijective bounded linear map on a non-Banach space can have unbounded inverse.

Uniform Boundedness PrincipleAdvanced
A sequence of bounded linear functionals fₙ:X→ℝ on a Banach space X with fₙ(x)→0 for every x implies:
A‖fₙ‖→0 (uniform convergence)
Bsup_n ‖fₙ‖<∞ (uniformly bounded)
C‖fₙ‖≥1 for all n
Dfₙ=0 for large n
✓ Correct Answer: (B) — sup_n ‖fₙ‖ < ∞
Banach-Steinhaus (Uniform Boundedness Principle): if {Tₙ}⊂B(X,Y) with sup_n‖Tₙx‖<∞ for every x, then sup_n‖Tₙ‖<∞. Here fₙ(x)→0 implies sup_n|fₙ(x)|<∞ for each x → sup_n‖fₙ‖<∞ (option B). Note: pointwise convergence does NOT imply ‖fₙ‖→0 (uniform convergence is stronger). Example: fₙ(x)=nx₁ on c₀: pointwise→0 but ‖fₙ‖=n→∞ — wait, this contradicts B. No: if fₙ(x)→0 for each x on a Banach space, then by B-S the norms are bounded, but need not go to 0.

Compact OperatorAdvanced
A compact operator K on an infinite-dimensional Banach space X cannot be:
ABounded
BThe limit of finite-rank operators
CA bijection (invertible)
DSelf-adjoint (if X is a Hilbert space)
✓ Correct Answer: (C) — A bijection (invertible)
A compact operator K on an infinite-dimensional Banach space cannot be invertible (bijective). Proof: if K were bijective, K⁻¹ would be bounded (by bounded inverse theorem). Then I=K⁻¹∘K would be compact (composition of bounded and compact). But the identity on an infinite-dim space is not compact (the unit ball is not compact by Riesz's theorem). Contradiction. (A) is possible: all compact operators are bounded. (B) is possible: on Hilbert spaces, all compact = limit of finite-rank (approximation property). (D) is possible: there exist compact self-adjoint operators (diagonalizable with eigenvalues→0).
