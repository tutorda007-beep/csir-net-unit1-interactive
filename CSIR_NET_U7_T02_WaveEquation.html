The Wave Equation
d'Alembert's formula · Separation of variables · Domain of influence · Finite speed propagation
d'Alembert Standing Waves Finite Speed Domain of Influence Separation of Variables Fourier Series

Introduction

The wave equation u_tt = c²u_xx models vibrating strings, sound waves, and electromagnetic waves. It is hyperbolic, and solutions propagate at finite speed c. Key feature: information travels along the characteristics x±ct=const.

1D Wave Equation

Cauchy Problem (Infinite String)
u_tt = c²u_xx, x ∈ ℝ, t > 0 u(x,0) = f(x) (initial displacement) u_t(x,0) = g(x) (initial velocity)
IBVP (Finite String)
u_tt = c²u_xx, 0 < x < L, t > 0 u(0,t) = 0, u(L,t) = 0 (Dirichlet BCs) u(x,0) = f(x), u_t(x,0) = g(x)
Factorization

u_tt−c²u_xx = (∂_t−c∂_x)(∂_t+c∂_x)u = 0.

New variables ξ=x−ct, η=x+ct convert to u_ξη=0, giving general solution u=F(x−ct)+G(x+ct) (superposition of right-traveling and left-traveling waves).

d'Alembert's Formula

d'Alembert's Formula (Cauchy Problem)
u(x,t) = [f(x−ct)+f(x+ct)]/2 + (1/2c)∫_{x−ct}^{x+ct} g(s)ds

This is the unique solution to the Cauchy problem. It is valid for all (x,t) with t≥0, requiring f∈C² and g∈C¹.

Domain of Dependence

u(x₀,t₀) depends only on initial data in the interval [x₀−ct₀, x₀+ct₀]. The domain of dependence is this interval on the initial line.

Domain of Influence

The data at x₀ affects u(x,t) only in the region |x−x₀| ≤ ct (inside the forward light cone). Signals travel at speed at most c.

Example

f(x)=sin(x), g(x)=0, c=1: u=(sin(x−t)+sin(x+t))/2=sin(x)cos(t). Standing wave!

Separation of Variables (Finite String)

Method

Assume u(x,t)=X(x)T(t). Substituting: T″X = c²X″T ⟹ T″/(c²T) = X″/X = −λ.

BCs: X(0)=X(L)=0 ⟹ λ_n=(nπ/L)², X_n=sin(nπx/L). For each n: T_n=A_n cos(nπct/L)+B_n sin(nπct/L).

Full Solution (Fourier Series)
u(x,t) = Σ_{n=1}^∞ sin(nπx/L)[Aₙcos(nπct/L) + Bₙsin(nπct/L)] Aₙ = (2/L)∫₀ᴸ f(x)sin(nπx/L)dx Bₙ = (2/nπc)∫₀ᴸ g(x)sin(nπx/L)dx

Each term sin(nπx/L)cos(nπct/L) is a standing wave with frequency ω_n=nπc/L and wavelength 2L/n.

Normal Modes

The nth mode has frequency ωₙ=nπc/L (rad/s) or fₙ=nc/(2L) Hz. The fundamental frequency is f₁=c/(2L). Higher modes are overtones (harmonics).

Energy & Domain of Influence

Energy Conservation
E(t) = (1/2)∫₀ᴸ [u_t² + c²u_x²] dx = const

The energy (kinetic + potential) is conserved. This proves uniqueness: if f=g=0 then E(0)=0 ⟹ E(t)=0 ⟹ u=0.

Finite Speed of Propagation

If f and g are supported in [a,b], then u(x,t)=0 outside [a−ct,b+ct]. Information travels at speed exactly c.

Huygens' Principle (odd dimensions)

In 3D, disturbances propagate exactly on the light cone (not inside). In 2D (even dimensions), disturbances persist inside the cone — "afterglow" effect. 1D: half-Huygens (d'Alembert formula has a continuous part).

Summary

General Solution

u=F(x−ct)+G(x+ct). Right/left traveling waves superimposed.

d'Alembert

u=[f(x−ct)+f(x+ct)]/2 + (1/2c)∫g. Requires f∈C², g∈C¹.

Domain of Dependence

u(x₀,t₀) uses data on [x₀−ct₀,x₀+ct₀]. Finite speed = c.

Separation

Modes sin(nπx/L)[Aₙcos(ωₙt)+Bₙsin(ωₙt)], ωₙ=nπc/L.

Energy

E=∫[u_t²+c²u_x²]dx/2 = constant. Proves uniqueness.

Huygens

Holds (sharp wavefront) in odd dims ≥3. 2D: tail persists.
