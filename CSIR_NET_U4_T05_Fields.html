Introduction

Fields are rings where every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. Field extensions (larger fields containing a given field) arise when we adjoin roots of polynomials. The theory of field extensions — culminating in Galois theory — answers classical questions like: "Which polynomials are solvable by radicals?"

Fields: Basic Definitions

Field

A field is a commutative ring with unity where every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse.

Equivalently: F is a field iff F* = F\{0} is an abelian group under multiplication.

Characteristic of a Field

The characteristic char(F) is the smallest n≥1 with n·1=0, or 0 if no such n exists.

char(F) is always 0 or a prime p. Examples: char(ℚ)=char(ℝ)=char(ℂ)=0; char(𝔽ₚ)=p; char(𝔽_{p^n})=p.

Prime Subfield

Every field F contains a unique smallest subfield called its prime subfield:

  • If char(F)=0: prime subfield ≅ ℚ
  • If char(F)=p: prime subfield ≅ 𝔽ₚ

Field Extensions

Field Extension

K/F (read "K over F") is a field extension if F is a subfield of K. K is an F-vector space, and its dimension [K:F] is the degree of the extension.

Finite extension: [K:F] < ∞. Algebraic extension: every element of K is algebraic over F.

Tower Law (Multiplicativity of Degrees)
[K:F] = [K:E] · [E:F]

for field tower F ⊆ E ⊆ K.

Simple Extensions

F(α) = smallest field extension of F containing α. If p(x) = min poly of α over F (monic irreducible of degree n), then:

F(α) ≅ F[x]/(p(x)) and [F(α):F] = deg p(x) = n

Basis: {1, α, α², ..., αⁿ⁻¹}.

Algebraic Extensions

Algebraic Element

α ∈ K is algebraic over F if f(α)=0 for some nonzero f∈F[x].

The minimal polynomial of α over F = the unique monic irreducible polynomial in F[x] of which α is a root.

α is transcendental over F if no such polynomial exists (e.g., π, e over ℚ).

Algebraic Closure

Every field F has an algebraic closure F̄ — the unique (up to isomorphism) algebraically closed field extension of F in which every polynomial over F splits completely.

ℂ is the algebraic closure of ℝ. The algebraic closure of ℚ is Q̄ ⊂ ℂ (algebraic numbers).

Finite Fields

Classification of Finite Fields

For every prime p and positive integer n, there exists a unique (up to isomorphism) field with pⁿ elements, denoted 𝔽_{pⁿ} or GF(pⁿ).

These are the only finite fields. |𝔽_{pⁿ}| = pⁿ and char(𝔽_{pⁿ}) = p.

Key Properties of 𝔽_{pⁿ}
  • 𝔽_{pⁿ} is the splitting field of xᵖⁿ − x over 𝔽ₚ.
  • The multiplicative group 𝔽*_{pⁿ} is cyclic of order pⁿ−1.
  • [𝔽_{pⁿ} : 𝔽ₚ] = n.
  • 𝔽_{pᵐ} ⊆ 𝔽_{pⁿ} ⟺ m | n.
  • Frobenius automorphism: σ: x ↦ xᵖ is a field automorphism of order n.

Galois Theory Overview

Galois Group

For field extension K/F: the Galois group Gal(K/F) = {σ: K→K field automorphism with σ|_F = id_F}.

|Gal(K/F)| ≤ [K:F]. Equality holds when K/F is a Galois extension (normal + separable).

Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

For a finite Galois extension K/F with G = Gal(K/F), there is a bijection (Galois correspondence):

{subgroups H ≤ G} ←→ {intermediate fields F ⊆ E ⊆ K}

Via H ↦ K^H (fixed field) and E ↦ Gal(K/E). The correspondence reverses inclusions:

  • H₁ ≤ H₂ ⟺ K^{H₁} ⊇ K^{H₂}
  • [K^H : F] = [G:H], [K:K^H] = |H|
  • E/F is Galois ⟺ Gal(K/E) ◁ G
Solvability by Radicals

A polynomial f ∈ F[x] is solvable by radicals iff the Galois group of its splitting field is a solvable group.

The general polynomial of degree ≥ 5 is NOT solvable by radicals (Galois group = S₅ which is not solvable). This is the Abel-Ruffini theorem.
