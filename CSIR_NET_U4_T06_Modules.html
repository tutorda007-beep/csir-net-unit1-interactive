Introduction

Modules are the natural generalization of vector spaces, where the scalar field is replaced by a ring. Every abelian group is a ℤ-module; every vector space is an F-module (F a field). The structure theorem for finitely generated modules over PIDs unifies the classification of abelian groups and the theory of the Jordan/rational canonical form.

Modules: Basic Definitions

Left R-Module

A left R-module M is an abelian group (M,+) with a ring R acting on M via R × M → M, (r,m) ↦ rm, satisfying:

  1. r(m+n) = rm+rn
  2. (r+s)m = rm+sm
  3. (rs)m = r(sm)
  4. 1·m = m (if R has unity)

Module Homomorphism

φ: M → N is an R-module homomorphism if φ(m+n)=φ(m)+φ(n) and φ(rm)=rφ(m).

ker φ is a submodule of M; im φ is a submodule of N.

For vector spaces (R=F), module homomorphisms are linear maps.

Submodules & Quotient Modules

Submodule

N ≤ M (N is a submodule of M) if N is a subgroup of M closed under R-action: rn∈N for all r∈R, n∈N.

For vector spaces: submodules = subspaces. For ℤ-modules (abelian groups): submodules = subgroups. For R as R-module: submodules = ideals.

Quotient Module

For submodule N ≤ M: M/N = {m+N : m∈M} is an R-module with r(m+N) = rm+N.

First Isomorphism Theorem (Modules)
M/ker φ ≅ im φ

for any R-module homomorphism φ: M → N.

Noetherian Modules

An R-module M is Noetherian if every ascending chain of submodules stabilizes: N₁⊆N₂⊆... ⟹ Nₖ=Nₖ₊₁=... for some k.

Equivalently: every submodule of M is finitely generated.

ℤ is Noetherian (Hilbert basis theorem: if R is Noetherian, so is R[x]).

Free & Projective Modules

Free Module

M is free of rank n if M ≅ Rⁿ as R-modules (basis of n elements).

Properties: every element has a unique representation as a finite linear combination of basis elements.

Vector spaces over a field are always free. Free ℤ-modules are free abelian groups ≅ ℤⁿ.

Projective and Injective Modules

Projective: P such that every surjection N→P splits (P is a direct summand of any module surjecting onto it). Direct summand of a free module.

Injective: I such that every injection I→N splits. Over PID: injective ⟺ divisible.

Over a field: all modules are both projective and injective (= vector spaces).

Structure Theorem for Finitely Generated Modules

Structure Theorem over PIDs

Every finitely generated module M over a PID R is isomorphic to:

M ≅ Rᵣ ⊕ R/(a₁) ⊕ R/(a₂) ⊕ ... ⊕ R/(aₖ)

where r ≥ 0 (free rank), and a₁|a₂|...|aₖ (invariant factors, each non-unit).

Alternatively (primary decomposition): M ≅ Rʳ ⊕ R/(p₁^{e₁}) ⊕ ... ⊕ R/(pₘ^{eₘ}) where the pᵢ are (not necessarily distinct) primes.

Applications
  • Finite abelian groups (R=ℤ): Every finite abelian group G ≅ ℤ_{n₁}⊕...⊕ℤ_{nₖ} with n₁|n₂|...|nₖ. Equivalently ≅ ℤ_{p^{a₁}}⊕...⊕ℤ_{p^{aₘ}} (primary decomposition).
  • Linear algebra (R=F[x]): A vector space V with linear operator T is an F[x]-module. Structure theorem gives Jordan canonical form and rational canonical form.
Classification of Finite Abelian Groups

Up to isomorphism, every abelian group of order n is determined by its invariant factors d₁|d₂|...|dₖ with d₁d₂···dₖ=n, or equivalently by the prime power decomposition.

Example: groups of order 12: ℤ₁₂≅ℤ₄×ℤ₃ or ℤ₂×ℤ₂×ℤ₃≅ℤ₂×ℤ₆. Two non-isomorphic groups of order 12 (abelian): ℤ₁₂ and ℤ₂×ℤ₆.
