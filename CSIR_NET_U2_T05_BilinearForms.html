Introduction

A bilinear form is a function B: V×V → F that is linear in each argument. Quadratic forms arise as Q(v) = B(v,v) for symmetric bilinear forms. Their classification — by Sylvester's law of inertia — is a cornerstone of linear algebra with applications in optimization, geometry, and physics.

Bilinear Forms

Definition

B: V×V → F is a bilinear form if for all u,v,w ∈ V and α,β ∈ F:

  • B(αu+βv, w) = αB(u,w) + βB(v,w) (linear in first argument)
  • B(u, αv+βw) = αB(u,v) + βB(u,w) (linear in second argument)

Symmetric: B(u,v) = B(v,u). Skew-symmetric: B(u,v) = −B(v,u). Alternating: B(v,v) = 0 for all v.

Matrix Representation

Gram Matrix

Fix a basis {e₁,...,eₙ} of V. The Gram matrix of B is A = [aᵢⱼ] where aᵢⱼ = B(eᵢ,eⱼ). Then:

B(u,v) = uᵀAv = Σᵢⱼ aᵢⱼ uᵢvⱼ

B is symmetric iff A = Aᵀ. B is skew-symmetric iff A = −Aᵀ.

Change of Basis

Under a change of basis x = Py (P invertible), the Gram matrix transforms as:

A' = PᵀAP

Two bilinear forms are congruent if their Gram matrices are related by A' = PᵀAP for some invertible P. Congruence is the right equivalence for bilinear forms (vs. similarity B=P⁻¹AP for linear maps).

Quadratic Forms

Definition

A quadratic form on ℝⁿ is a function Q: ℝⁿ → ℝ:

Q(x) = xᵀAx = Σᵢⱼ aᵢⱼ xᵢxⱼ

where A is a symmetric matrix. We may always take A symmetric: given any M, Q(x) = xᵀMx = xᵀ[(M+Mᵀ)/2]x, and (M+Mᵀ)/2 is symmetric.

Examples

ℝ² form

Q(x,y) = ax² + 2bxy + cy². Matrix: [[a,b],[b,c]].

ℝ³ form

Q(x,y,z) = ax²+by²+cz²+2dxy+2exz+2fyz. Matrix with off-diagonal halved.

Completing the Square

Q(x,y) = x² + 4xy + y² = (x+2y)² − 3y². Let u=x+2y, v=y: Q = u² − 3v². This diagonalizes the form.

Canonical (Normal) Form

Sylvester's Law of Inertia

Every real quadratic form Q(x) = xᵀAx (A symmetric) can be reduced by a congruence transformation (non-singular substitution x=Py) to its canonical form:

Q = y₁² + y₂² + ... + yₚ² − yₚ₊₁² − ... − yₚ₊ᵧ²

The numbers p (positive squares) and q (negative squares) are invariants — they do not depend on the choice of diagonalizing basis. This is Sylvester's law of inertia.

Reduction Methods

Via Eigenvalues

Diagonalize A = QDQᵀ. The signs of diagonal entries of D give p, q. Then x = Qy transforms Q into the sum of squares form.

Via Completing Square

Eliminate cross terms by substitution. Count positive and negative squared terms. Works without full diagonalization for simple forms.

Sylvester's Criterion (Positive Definiteness)

A symmetric matrix A is positive definite iff all leading principal minors Δ₁, Δ₂, ..., Δₙ are positive:

Δ₁ = a₁₁ > 0, Δ₂ = det([[a₁₁,a₁₂],[a₂₁,a₂₂]]) > 0, ..., Δₙ = det(A) > 0
General Definiteness via Minors

For negative definite: Δₖ has sign (−1)ᵏ (alternating starting with −). For indefinite: minors don't fit the above patterns. For semidefinite: all principal minors (not just leading) ≥ 0.

Common Mistake

det(A)>0 and tr(A)>0 does NOT guarantee positive definiteness for n≥3. You must check ALL leading principal minors, not just det and tr.

Symplectic & Hermitian Forms

Symplectic Forms

Definition

A symplectic form (non-degenerate alternating bilinear form) on V (dim=2n) satisfies:

  • ω(u,v) = −ω(v,u) (skew-symmetric)
  • ω(u,v)=0 for all v ⟹ u=0 (non-degenerate)

Standard example on ℝ²ⁿ: ω(x,y) = Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (xᵢyₙ₊ᵢ − xₙ₊ᵢyᵢ).

All symplectic forms on ℝ²ⁿ are equivalent (Darboux's theorem): canonical form ω(x,y) = xᵀJy where J = [[0,Iₙ],[−Iₙ,0]].

Hermitian Forms (Complex Case)

Definition

H: V×V → ℂ is a Hermitian form if H(u,v) = H̄(v,u) (conjugate symmetric) and linear in first argument. H is positive definite if H(v,v)>0 for v≠0. Matrix form: H(u,v) = u*Mv with M = M* (Hermitian matrix).

Classification of Hermitian forms over ℂ: same as real quadratic forms — Sylvester's law of inertia applies (p positive, q negative eigenvalues).
